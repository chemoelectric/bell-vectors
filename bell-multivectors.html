Multivectors and geometric algebra

Vector products and grade

Ordinary Gibbs-Heaviside vectors are nice enough, but, like physicists, we want to impress the plebeians. Physicists do this by using complex numbers to represent rotations in a plane, but are not complex numbers are more properly roots of univariate real polynomials? A much more general set of methods is geometric algebra, from which we will draw just a miniscule subset of what it contains and can do.

In geometric algebra, you can add a scalar and a vector. At least if you are as old as I am, you are likely to have an acquaintance who will argue with you vociferously that you cannot add a scalar and a vector. Such an acquaintance is a person who has obtained a high school or university education, but has not overcome that education. It is important to obtain an education, but current educations are very bad, and so it is necessary also to overcome the damage done. A school education has solidified the brain rather than made it more plastic, and also much of what is taught is simply wrong, or limited to a context but taught as if it were universal.

You can also multiply vectors in ways other than dot and cross products. The geometric product \(uv\) of vectors \(u\) and \(v\) is

\begin{equation} uv = u\cdot v + u\wedge v \end{equation}

where \(u\cdot v\) is the familiar dot product, but the wedge product \(u\wedge v\) is a new entity called a bivector. A bivector is a finite planar area that is oriented either counterclockwise or clockwise. You can picture a bivector as an entity normal to which is the familiar cross product.

The sum of any scalars, vectors, and bivectors is called a multivector.

Let us also introduce the grade operator, which returns the scalar (grade 0), vector (grade 1), or bivector (grade 2) part of a multivector:

\begin{align*} {\left\langle{5 + 2e_1 + 7e_1\wedge e_2}\right\rangle}_0 &= 5 \\ {\left\langle{5 + 2e_1 + 7e_1\wedge e_2}\right\rangle}_1 &= 2e_1 \\ {\left\langle{5 + 2e_1 + 7e_1\wedge e_2}\right\rangle}_2 &= 7e_1\wedge e_2 \\ \end{align*}

Given the grade operation, the dot and wedge products of two vectors can be obtained from their geometric product:

\begin{align*} u\cdot v &= {\left\langle uv\right\rangle}_0 \\ u\wedge v &= {\left\langle uv\right\rangle}_2 \end{align*}

Here are some identities:

\begin{align*} u\cdot v &= \lVert u\rVert\lVert v\rVert &\text{if } u\parallel v \\ u\wedge v &= 0 &\text{if } u\parallel v \\ u\cdot v &= 0 &\text{if } u\perp v \\ u\wedge v = uv = -vu &= -v\wedge u &\text{if } u\perp v \\ u\cdot v &= v\cdot u & \text{always} \\ u\wedge v &= -v\wedge u & \text{always} \end{align*}

Basis vectors

Suppose we want to have an \((x,y)\) coordinate system for geometric algebra. We can do this by defining basis vectors \(e_1\) of magnitude 1 in the \(x\)-direction and \(e_2\) of magnitude 1 in the \(y\)-direction.

The following are useful identities:

\begin{align*} e_1\cdot e_2 &= e_2\cdot e_1 = 0 \\ e_1\wedge e_1 &= e_2\wedge e_2 = 0 \\ e_1\cdot e_1 &= e_1 e_1 = e_2 e_2 = e_2\cdot e_2 = 1 \\ e_1\wedge e_2 &= e_1 e_2 = -e_2 e_1 = -e_2\wedge e_1 \end{align*}

Rotors

What if you need a unit vector other than one of the basis vectors? Complex numbers around the clock face are obtained by rotating the number 1: the number 1 is rotated counterclockwise by an angle \(\varphi\) by multiplying it by \(e^{i\varphi} = \cos\varphi + i\sin\varphi\).

There is an analogous operation in geometric algebra. For this operation, one uses a rotor. To rotate counterclockwise by \(\varphi\), the rotor and its inverse are

\begin{align*} R_\varphi &= \cos(\varphi/2) - e_1 e_2 \sin(\varphi/2) \\ R_\varphi^{-1} &= \cos(\varphi/2) + e_1 e_2 \sin(\varphi/2) \end{align*}

Any vector around the clock face can be gotten by rotating \(e_1\), thus:

\begin{align*} e_\varphi &= R_\varphi \, e_1 \, R_\varphi^{-1} \\ &= (\cos\varphi' - e_1 e_2 \sin\varphi') \, e_1 \, (\cos\varphi' + e_1 e_2 \sin\varphi') \\ &= e_1 \cos^2\varphi' - e_1 e_2 e_1 \cos\varphi'\sin\varphi' + e_1 e_1 e_2 \cos\varphi'\sin\varphi' - e_1 e_2 e_1 e_1 e_2 \sin^2\varphi' \end{align*}

where \(\varphi' = \varphi/2\). Identities mentioned above allow simplifications:

\begin{align*} -e_1 e_2 e_1 &= e_2 \\ e_1 e_1 e_2 &= e_2 \\ - e_1 e_2 e_1 e_1 e_2 &= -e_1 \end{align*}

Therefore,

\begin{equation} e_\varphi = (\cos^2\varphi' - \sin^2\varphi')\,e_1 + (2\,\cos\varphi'\sin\varphi')\,e_2 \end{equation}

Let us refer to the CRC Handbook of Mathematical Sciences, 6th Edition, where we find the following double-angle relations on page 170:

\begin{align*} \sin 2\alpha &= 2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha \\ \cos 2\alpha &= \cos^2\alpha - \sin^2\alpha \end{align*}

Therefore,

\begin{align*} e_\varphi &= e_1\cos\varphi + e_2\sin\varphi \end{align*}

or, in other words, we can use trigonometry and component addition. Which many readers already knew, but now we have impressed the plebeians with rotors and our possession of a massive reference book from yesteryear.

We have also shown by implication that using complex numbers is the same thing: impressing the plebeians with mysteriously named entities “complex” and “imaginary” numbers, when actually you are doing trigonometry and component addition.

Polarizing beam splitters (PBS)

The effect of a polarizing filter on plane-polarized light is described by the Law of Malus: the intensity of the retransmitted light is reduced by a factor of \(\cos^2(\vartheta-\varphi)\), where \(\vartheta\) is the angle of the filter’s axis of transmission and \(\varphi\) is the angle of polarization of the impinging light. Let us suppose the mechanism of light is such that a pulse either is or is not retransmitted. In that case intensity can be equated with probability of retransmission of a light pulse.

Now let us proceed from simple polarizing filters to polarizing beam splitters (PBS), also known as beam-splitting polarizers. These are devices made, one way or another, from two complementary polarizing filters, that split a light beam into complementary polarizations. In one channel the light will be polarized along the main axis of transmission, and in the other channel along the complementary axis, which is normal to the main axis. Ideally, the intensity of light in the complementary channel is reduced by the factor \(\sin^2(\vartheta-\varphi)\), so the total intensity is not lost. Similarly, for our probabilistic interpretation, we will make an idealistic assumption: that the light pulse is retransmitted in either the main or the complementary channel, but never lost. The probability of retransmission is \( \cos^2 (\vartheta - \varphi) + \sin^2 (\vartheta - \varphi) = 1 \).

Sometimes I visualize the action of a polarizing filter as the projection of the vector of the impinging light onto the axis of polarization of the filter. This gives a vector of magnitude reduced from unity, and the square of that magnitude is the intensity of the retransmitted light. If \(e_\vartheta\) is a unit vector representing the axis of transmission of the filter, and \(e_\varphi\) the angle of polarization of the impinging light, then the projection is

\begin{equation} \text{proj}_{e_\vartheta}e_\varphi = (e_\varphi\cdot e_\vartheta)e_\vartheta \end{equation}

The probability of retransmission is, conveniently, the geometric product of this projection with itself:

\begin{equation} (\text{proj}_{e_\vartheta}e_\varphi)^2 = (e_\varphi\cdot e_\vartheta)^2 e_\vartheta e_\vartheta = \cos^2 (\vartheta - \varphi) \end{equation}

Above we have presumed light plane-polarized either \(e_1\) or \(e_2\), and there are two polarizers, the main channel \(e_\vartheta\) and the complementary channel \(e_{\vartheta+\pi/2}\). Therefore there are the following special instances:

\begin{align*} (\text{proj}_{e_\vartheta}e_1)^2 &= (e_1\cdot e_\vartheta)^2 e_\vartheta^2 = \cos^2\vartheta \\ (\text{proj}_{e_\vartheta}e_2)^2 &= (e_2\cdot e_\vartheta)^2 e_\vartheta^2 = \sin^2\vartheta \\ (\text{proj}_{e_{\vartheta+\pi/2}}e_1)^2 &= (e_1\cdot e_{\vartheta+\pi/2})^2 e_{\vartheta+\pi/2}^2 = \cos^2(\vartheta+\pi/2) = \sin^2\vartheta \\ (\text{proj}_{e_{\vartheta+\pi/2}}e_2)^2 &= (e_2\cdot e_{\vartheta+\pi/2})^2 e_{\vartheta+\pi/2}^2 = \sin^2(\vartheta+\pi/2) = \cos^2\vartheta \end{align*}

The apparatus of the experiment has two PBS. Let us call the angles of the main axes of transmission \(\zeta\) (zeta) for the left PBS and \(\eta\) (eta) for the right PBS. For the complementary channels, the angles are \(\zeta+\pi/2\) and \(\eta+\pi/2\). Thus on the left side will be either light pulse retransmitted can be described either as \(e_\zeta\) or \(e_{\zeta+\pi/2}\), depending on which channel was taken. Similarly, on the right side, the retransmitted light pulse is \(e_\eta\) or \(e_{\eta+\pi/2}\).

Thus the facts of the experiment, taking into account everything stated so far, can be written as a point in a tensor space. In the following proposition, \(P_1,P_2,P_3,P_4\) represent mutual probabilities of the possibilities represented by the matrix columns:

\begin{align*} \text{prop}_2 = &\space P_1\begin{pmatrix}e_\zeta&e_1\\e_\eta&e_2\end{pmatrix} + P_2\begin{pmatrix}e_{\zeta+\pi/2}&e_1\\e_\eta&e_2\end{pmatrix} \\ &+ P_2\begin{pmatrix}e_\zeta&e_1\\e_{\eta+\pi/2}&e_2\end{pmatrix} + P_2\begin{pmatrix}e_{\zeta+\pi/2} &e_1\\e_{\eta+\pi/2}&e_2\end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

Again the upper rows represent the left side of the apparatus and the lower rows represent the right side.

The probability of \(\text{prop}_1 = {\begin{pmatrix}e_1&e_2\end{pmatrix}}^t\) is one, and thus each of \(P_1,P_2,P_3,P_4\) equals the conditional probability of the left column, given the facts described by the right column (\(\text{prop}_1\)). This is true by the definition of the conditional probability,

\begin{equation} \text{P}(\text{prop}_x|\text{prop}_y) = \frac{\text{P}(\text{prop}_x\wedge\text{prop}_y)}{ \text{P}(\text{prop}_y)} \end{equation}

where in this case \( \text{P}(\text{prop}_y) = \text{P}(\text{prop}_1) = 1 \).

Although John S. Bell famously interpreted conditional probability as meaning “no action at a distance,” and uses this interpretation to justify eliding facts from the EPR-B experiment (whereas we, above, made certain to include every fact), conditional probability has no such meaning. The mathematical definition of conditional probability is exactly what is written above. You can look this up in a text on probability theory. You can also verify, though, by playing with collections of ordinary objects, that conditional probability as defined above has the following intuitive meaning: it is the probability \(\text{prop}_x\) is true, given that \(\text{prop}_y\) is true.

This means we calculate \(P_1,P_2,P_3,P_4\) by assuming \(e_1\) on the left and \(e_2\) on the right. We would have done so anyway, but it is nice to see the mathematics even subtly agreeing with us.

A formula for computing the probabilities is as follows. If

\begin{equation} M_n = \begin{pmatrix} e_{\angle a} & e_1 \\ e_{\angle b} & e_2 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation}

then

\begin{equation} P_n \stackrel{?}{=} (\text{proj}_{e_{\angle a}}e_1)^2 (\text{proj}_{e_{\angle b}}e_2)^2 \end{equation}

which is the mutual probability of the top row and bottom row. We do not have to do probability theory calculations to eliminate \(e_1\) and \(e_2\), because the projection operations already have eliminated these vectors. Furthermore, the probability of one polarizer setting would seem not to be conditional on the probability of the other polarizer setting, because both these settings are arbitrary. Thus computing \(P_n\) is simply multiplying the probabilities of the two rows.

Or is it? We will see later on that it is not so simple. In probability theory, when there seems to be an impediment, there probably is an impediment.

Now \(\text{prop}_2\) can be written out more fully, but with doubt that what we are writing is correct:

\begin{align*} \text{prop}_2 = &\space P_1\begin{pmatrix}e_\zeta&e_1\\e_\eta&e_2\end{pmatrix} + P_2\begin{pmatrix}e_{\zeta+\pi/2}&e_1\\e_\eta&e_2\end{pmatrix} \\ &+ P_2\begin{pmatrix}e_\zeta&e_1\\e_{\eta+\pi/2}&e_2\end{pmatrix} + P_2\begin{pmatrix}e_{\zeta+\pi/2} &e_1\\e_{\eta+\pi/2}&e_2\end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

where

\begin{align*} P_1 &\stackrel{?}{=} \cos^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta \\ P_2 &\stackrel{?}{=} \sin^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta \\ P_3 &\stackrel{?}{=} \cos^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta \\ P_4 &\stackrel{?}{=} \sin^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta \end{align*}

The tensor \(\text{prop}_2\) is a proposition stating the probability of each possible event in the two-channel Bell test experiment, in terms of \(\zeta\) and \(\eta\). Be very careful, though, in how you evaluate that! Those angles are defined relative to \(e_1\), and \(e_1\) is itself pointed in some unstated direction. We do not yet have a suitable result.

We do, however, have a result that gives a probability for every possible event in the experiment and which can be checked for summation to one:

\begin{align*} P_1 + P_2 + P_3 + P_4 &= \cos^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta + \sin^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta \\ &\quad\quad + \cos^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta + \sin^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta \\ &= (\cos^2\zeta)(\sin^2\eta + \cos^2\eta) \\ &\quad\quad + (\sin^2\zeta)(\sin^2\eta + \cos^2\eta) \\ &= \cos^2\zeta + \sin^2\zeta \\ &= 1 \end{align*}

Correlation

Expectation

Let us make a small diversion onto topics of probability theory. The first will be expectation.

An expectation is an average weighted by probabilities, by frequencies of occurrence, or by some similar fractions. For example, suppose there are probabilities \(P_i\) that add to one, and associated numbers \(f(i)\). Then the expectation of \(f\) is

\begin{equation} \text{E}(f) = \sum_i P_i f(i) \end{equation}

Variance and standard deviation

Given the expectation of \(f\), it would seem interesting to measure the difference between \(f(i)\) and the expectation, but ignoring whether the difference is negative or positive. The sign can be ignored by squaring the difference.

Thus we have the variance of \(f\):

\begin{align*} \text{Var}(f) = \sigma_f^2 &= \text{E}\left((f - \text{E}(f))^2\right) \\ &= \sum_i\left(P_i\,\left(f(i) - \sum_{i'}P_{i'} f({i'})\right)^2\right) \end{align*}

The square root of the variance has the same dimensions as \(f\) and is called the standard deviation:

\begin{equation} \sigma_f = \sqrt{\text{Var}(f)} \end{equation}

Covariance and correlation

If there are two sets of numbers, \(f(i)\) and \(g(j)\), it can be useful to compute a figure analogous to the variance, but involving both sets. Such a figure is called the covariance:

\begin{align*} \text{cov}_{fg} = \sigma_{fg} &= \text{E}\left(\mkern1mu\left(f-\text{E}(f)\right) \left(g-\text{E}(g)\right)\mkern1mu\right) \\ &= \sum_{i,j} P_{ij} \left(f(i) - \sum_{i'}P_{i'} f({i'})\right) \left(g(j) - \sum_{j'}P_{j'} g({j'})\right) \end{align*}

A dimensionless figure called the correlation is obtained by dividing the covariance of \(f\) and \(g\) by the respective standard deviations:

\begin{equation} \text{corr}_{fg} = \rho_{fg} = \frac{\sigma_{fg}}{\sigma_f\,\sigma_g} \end{equation}

For the two-channel optical Bell test, there is a dimensionless figure physicists call the “quantum correlation” that equals \(-\cos2(\alpha-\beta)\), where \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are the polarizer settings. If physicists are correct, this figure characterizes the supposedly “spooky action at a distance” of the experiment, and should not be derivable without quantum mechanics. Yet below we will derive it as the ordinary correlation, even though we use no quantum mechanics and obviously assume only action by direct contact.[1]

The correlation of the two-channel optical Bell test

Solving two problems at once

Let

\begin{align*} M_1 &= \begin{pmatrix}e_\zeta&e_1\\e_\eta&e_2\end{pmatrix} & M_2 &= \begin{pmatrix}e_{\zeta+\pi/2}&e_1\\e_\eta&e_2\end{pmatrix} \\ M_3 &= \begin{pmatrix}e_\zeta&e_1\\e_{\eta+\pi/2}&e_2\end{pmatrix} & M_4 &= \begin{pmatrix}e_{\zeta+\pi/2}&e_1\\e_{\eta+\pi/2}&e_2\end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

Then

\begin{equation} \text{prop}_2 = P_1M_1 + P_2M_2 + P_3M_3 + P_4M_4 \end{equation}

Define \(f\) as follows:

\begin{align*} f(M_1) &= f(M_3) &= +1 \\ f(M_2) &= f(M_4) &= -1 \end{align*}

Thus \(f\) equals \$+1\$ if the left PBS retransmits in its main channel, \$-1\$ if it retransmits in its complementary channel.

The expectation of \(f\) is

\begin{align*} \text{E}(f) &\stackrel{?}{=} \cos^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta - \sin^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta \\ &\quad\quad +\cos^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta - \sin^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta \\ &= \cos^2\zeta - \sin^2\zeta \end{align*}

which is the probability of a retransmission in the main channel minus the probability of a retransmission in the complementary channel.

(There is reason to believe this is a correct answer, despite that it was derived by questionable means. The reason is that \(\eta\) disappears from the answer.)

We would have liked for the expectation to equal zero. So let us use a trick.[2] Let us solve two problems simultaneously. Let us solve also for the case where the initial light pulses are \(\begin{pmatrix}e_2&e_1\end{pmatrix}^t\). Towards that end, introduce \(\text{prop}_3\) and let us analyze \(\text{prop}_3\), which has symmetry:

\begin{equation} \text{prop}_3 = \frac12\begin{pmatrix}e_1\\e_2\end{pmatrix} + \frac12\begin{pmatrix}e_2\\e_1\end{pmatrix} \end{equation}

Remember that in \(\text{prop}_1\) the axis of transmission was not specified. Thus \(\text{prop}_3\) is, in effect, the same problem as \(\text{prop}_1\). The new proposition says “the plane of polarization has one half probability of being a certain way, one half the complementary way, but in either case that plane is unspecified.” If we analyze that problem, we are analyzing the same problem as the original.

We now need four more matrices:

\begin{align*} M_5 &= \begin{pmatrix}e_\zeta&e_2\\e_\eta&e_1\end{pmatrix} & M_6 &= \begin{pmatrix}e_{\zeta+\pi/2}&e_2\\e_\eta&e_1\end{pmatrix} \\ M_7 &= \begin{pmatrix}e_\zeta&e_2\\e_{\eta+\pi/2}&e_1\end{pmatrix} & M_8 &= \begin{pmatrix}e_{\zeta+\pi/2}&e_2\\e_{\eta+\pi/2}&e_1\end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

And I will leave it as an exercise for the reader to show that the new (but still questionable) probability formula is

\begin{equation} P_n' \stackrel{?}{=} \begin{cases} \frac12(\text{proj}_{e_{\angle a}}e_1)^2 (\text{proj}_{e_{\angle b}}e_2)^2 & \text{if } n\in\{1,2,3,4\} \\ \frac12(\text{proj}_{e_{\angle a}}e_2)^2 (\text{proj}_{e_{\angle b}}e_1)^2 & \text{if } n\in\{5,6,7,8\} \end{cases} \end{equation}

so that

\begin{align*} P_1' &= P_8' \stackrel{?}{=} (1/2)\cos^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta \\ P_2' &= P_7' \stackrel{?}{=} (1/2)\sin^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta \\ P_3' &= P_6' \stackrel{?}{=} (1/2)\cos^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta \\ P_4' &= P_5' \stackrel{?}{=} (1/2)\sin^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta \end{align*}

Define

\begin{align*} f(M_5) &= f(M_7) &= +1 \\ f(M_6) &= f(M_8) &= -1 \end{align*}

so \(f\) still means \$+1\$ for the main channel. Then

\begin{align*} 2\,\text{E}(f) &\stackrel{?}{=} \cos^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta - \sin^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta \\ &\quad\quad +\cos^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta - \sin^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta \\ &\quad\quad\quad +\sin^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta -\cos^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta \\ &\quad\quad\quad\quad +\sin^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta -\cos^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta = 0 \end{align*}

A desirable value for the variance, and thus also of the standard deviation, is one. So let us see if we get that. The correlation is designed to eliminate the effects of the particular choices for functions such as \(f\), but that is what lets us choose our \(f\) to give us expectation and standard deviation that are easy to work with.

\begin{align*} 2\,\sigma_f^2 p &\stackrel{?}{=} (\cos^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta)\,(+1 - 0)^2 + (\sin^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta)\,(-1 - 0)^2\\ &\quad\quad +(\cos^2\zeta \, \cos^2 \eta)\,(+1 - 0)^2 + (\sin^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta)\,(-1 - 0)^2\\ &\quad\quad\quad +(\sin^2\zeta \, \cos^2 \eta)\,(+1 - 0)^2 +(\cos^2\zeta \, \cos^2\eta)\,(-1 - 0)^2\\ &\quad\quad\quad\quad +(\sin^2\zeta \, \sin^2 \eta)\,(+1 - 0)^2 +(\cos^2\zeta \, \sin^2\eta)\,(-1 - 0)^2 = 2 \end{align*}

Indeed, then, \(\sigma_f \stackrel{?}{=} 1\) as desired.

Define a function \(g\) that is similar to \(f\), but responds to the right-hand PBS instead of the left:

\begin{align*} f(M_1) &= f(M_3) = f(M_5) = f(M_7) &= +1 \\ f(M_2) &= f(M_4) = f(M_6) = f(M_8) &= -1 \\ g(M_1) &= g(M_2) = g(M_5) = g(M_6) &= +1 \\ g(M_3) &= g(M_4) = g(M_7) = g(M_8) &= -1 \end{align*}

The function \(g\), like \(f\), has expectation zero and standard deviation one.

On either side, which channel has positive sign and which negative is a matter of preference. With some settings the correlation will have a plus sign in front of a “\(\cos\)” and others a negative sign, and again it makes no real difference. Sometimes you will see it one way, sometimes the other. Our result will have a minus sign.

A naïve attempt to derive the correlation of \(f\) and \(g\)

Because the expectations of \(f\) and \(g\) are both zero, and their standard deviations are both one, the covariance and correlation of \(f\) and \(g\) are equal to each other. In general they would be different at least in that correlation is dimensionless, but our \(f\) and \(g\) already are dimensionless.

We can thus compute the covariance and treat the result as the correlation.

Let us try to derive the covariance of \(f\) and \(g\) by the formula

\begin{equation} \text{cov}_{fg} \stackrel{?}{=} \mkern-10mu \sum_{i,j\in {1,\ldots,8}} \mkern-10mu P_i P_j\,f(M_i) g(M_j) \end{equation}

This results in expressions similar to those of the CHSH inequality.

A little thought makes obvious a tremendous problem with this approach: the mathematics does not distinguish effects that are due to \(\zeta\) from those that are due to \(\eta\). Polarizers could be stuck willy-nilly anywhere within the apparatus, and the mathematics cannot tell the difference. We have made unwarranted assumptions about the mutual probabilities. We have failed to consider the mutual probabilities might be any of many different functions of \(\zeta\) and \(\eta\). The resulting expressions are useless.

The CHSH inequality also is useless.

What works is to specialize the problem so there is only one polarizer setting. Then that specialized solution can be generalized. (See [2])

A solution for \(\eta=0\)

It \(\eta=0\), then on the right side every \(e_1\) is retransmitted and every \(e_2\) is blocked. Thus the symbol \(\eta\) disappears from expressions. Physically, it is as if we put only a photodetector on that side of the apparatus, and had no polarizing filters on the right at all.

The difficulty with expressions containing both \(\zeta\) and \(\eta\) exists no longer. Effectively there is no right-side PBS. Now also all effects on the left are from the left-side PBS and are expressed through \(\zeta\).

We now have

\begin{align*} P_1' &= P_8' = 0 \\ P_2' &= P_7' = 0 \\ P_3' &= P_6' = (1/2)\cos^2\zeta \\ P_4' &= P_5' = (1/2)\sin^2\zeta \end{align*}

and can compute the correlation correctly:

\begin{align*} \rho_{fg} = \text{cov}_{fg} &= (1/2)\,(\cos^2\zeta)\,(f(M_3)g(M_3) + f(M_6)g(M_6)) \\ &\quad\quad + (1/2)\,(\sin^2\zeta)\,(f(M_4)g(M_4) + f(M_5)g(M_5)) \\ &= -(\cos^2\zeta - \sin^2\zeta) \\ &= -\cos2\mkern1.2mu\zeta \\ &= -\cos2(\zeta - \eta) \end{align*}

The next to last step is by one of the double-angle relations earlier quoted from the CRC Handbook of Mathematical Sciences. The last step is true because \(\eta=0\).

The result is the same as the “quantum correlation” for the given PBS settings. But there is still one more step before we get the complete answer.

Realizing that the particular polarization angle makes no difference

That step is to add zero to \(\zeta - \eta\).

But not just any zero. Let \(\delta\) be any angle whatsoever. Also let \(\alpha=\zeta+\delta\) and \(\beta=\eta+\delta\). Then

\begin{align*} -\cos2(\alpha-\beta) &= -\cos2((\zeta+\delta)-(\eta+\delta)) \\ &= -\cos2((\zeta-\eta)+(\delta-\delta)) \\ &= -\cos2((\zeta-\eta)+0) \\ &= -\cos2(\zeta-\eta) \end{align*}

Thus the PBS settings can be rotated together to any new calibration, without this rotation changing the form of the correlation. However, let us assume there is a standard calibration for PBS. We cannot recalibrated. There remains, however, an equivalent interpretation of adding zero this way: that the polarization angles of the initial light pulses do not matter towards the correlation, as long as the two planes of polarization are normal to each other.

This is because the correlation has turned out to be a function of relative angle, expressed as a cosine.

There is a factor of two that does not appear if the experiment were to be done with spin-1 particles. Mathematically, the only difference is a change of factor in the independent variables. Physically, I would give polarizing filters have the “projection” interpretation described above, whereas, perhaps, for spin-1 particles the device (such as a Stern-Gerlach magnet) is more like a rectangular window that is open only a certain amount. I base this interpretation on some investigation of trigonometric identities.

In any instance, the goal is achieved. I have derived the correlation of the two-channel optical Bell test, without quantum mechanics and assuming only action by direct contact. The correlation is

\begin{equation} \rho_{fg} = -\cos2(\alpha-\beta) \end{equation}

where \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are the respective axes of transmission of main channels the two PBS.

This is the same expression as the so-called “quantum correlation,” and it is correct, but we can see that “quantum” is a misnomer. It is the ordinary correlation from probability theory. It also can be derived from classical coherence theory of electromagnetic waves.[3] The only thing mysteriously “quantum” about the expression is how any “quantum” physicist obtained it by some means, given the field’s habit of uncritically publishing fallacious semi-solutions such as Bell’s and Clauser’s.
