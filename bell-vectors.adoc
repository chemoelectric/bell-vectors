To demonstrate that the silly Dirac notation is just a vector notation

Suppose we have a light source generating photons of complementary
plane polarizations. We do not specify in what planes they are
polarized, except that, of course, the planes are orthogonal to each
other. One photon will be represented in the Dirac notation by
stem:["→"] and the other by stem:["↑"]. Thus we can write bras
stem:["⟨→↑|"] and stem:["⟨↑→|"] and kets stem:["|→↑⟩"] and
stem:["|↑→⟩"]. Writing these liturgical notations turns the photons
into “an entangled pair.” But we will undo this “entanglement” by
defining mathematical notations.

Let us define the following. For one of the photons we write the
vector in a common notation for basis vectors, as stem:[e_1="→"]. The
other photon we write as an orthogonal basis vector thus:
stem:[e_2="↑"].

Notice, by the way, that this notation does not mislead us about the
direction of the plane polarization. In fact, the basis vectors are
agnostic about what distinguishes the photons, as long as the
distinction is one of orthogonality.

To further mathematize our notations, instead of bras we will write
row matrices and instead of kets we will write column matrices. Thus:

[stem]

[stem]
