dimensions to the tensor space. One now has to distinguish the two
paths at the exit of either beam splitter.

We will again use geometric algebra! We will use the dot product.
Fortunately for the proletarians, the dot product of two vectors in

We will also use the Law of Malus, equating the intensity of an
electromagnetic wave with the probability of occurrence of a photon.
The change in intensity due to a polarizing filter, according to the
Law of Malus, is a factor of

$$
\begin{equation}
\cos^2 (\theta - \phi) = (e_\theta\cdot e_\phi)^2
\end{equation}
$$

where stem:[theta] is the angle of polarization of the filter and
stem:[phi] is the angle of polarization of the impinging
plane-polarized light. For a lossless _beam-splitting_ polarizer, one
channel follows that rule and the other channel the complementary rule

$$
\begin{equation}
\sin^2(\theta - \phi) = 1 - (e_\theta\cdot e_\phi)^2
\end{equation}
$$

The impinging photon is polarized either stem:[e_1] or stem:[e_2].
Therefore the dot product is either stem:[e_theta*e_1 = cos theta] or
stem:[e_theta*e_2 = sin theta], and the loss of intensity according to
the Law of Malus is stem:[cos^2 theta] or stem:[sin^2 theta],

$$
\begin{equation}
1-(e_\theta\cdot e_1)^2 = 1-\cos^2\theta = \sin^2\theta
\end{equation}

$$
\begin{equation}
