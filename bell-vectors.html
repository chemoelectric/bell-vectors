Suppose we have a light source generating photons of complementary plane polarizations. We do not specify in what planes they are polarized, except that, of course, the planes are orthogonal to each other. One photon will be represented in the Dirac notation by \$"→"\$ and the other by \$"↑"\$. Thus we can write bras \$"⟨→↑|"\$ and \$"⟨↑→|"\$ and kets \$"|→↑⟩"\$ and \$"|↑→⟩"\$. Writing these liturgical notations turns the photons into “an entangled pair.” But we will undo this “entanglement” by defining mathematical notations.

Let us define the following. For one of the photons we write the vector in a common notation for basis vectors, as \$e_1="→"\$. The other photon we write as an orthogonal basis vector thus: \$e_2="↑"\$.

Notice, by the way, that this notation does not mislead us about the direction of the plane polarization. In fact, the basis vectors are agnostic about what distinguishes the photons, as long as the distinction is one of orthogonality.

To further mathematize our notations, instead of bras we will write row matrices and instead of kets we will write column matrices. Thus:

\begin{align*} \text{⟨→↑|} &= \begin{pmatrix}e_1 & e_2\end{pmatrix} \\ \text{⟨↑→|} &= \begin{pmatrix}e_2 & e_1\end{pmatrix} \\ \text{|→↑⟩} &= \begin{pmatrix}e_1 \\ e_2\end{pmatrix} \\ \text{|↑→⟩} &= \begin{pmatrix}e_2 \\ e_1\end{pmatrix} \\ \end{align*}

Let us review the Law of Malus, but equating the intensity of an electromagnetic wave with the probability of occurrence of a photon. The change in intensity due to a polarizing filter, according to the Law of Malus, is a factor

\begin{equation} \cos^2 (\vartheta - \varphi) = (e_\vartheta\cdot e_\varphi)^2 = {\lVert \text{proj}_{e_\vartheta}e_\varphi\rVert}^2 \end{equation}

where \$vartheta\$ is the angle of polarization of the filter and \$varphi\$ is the angle of polarization of the impinging plane-polarized light. Here \(\text{proj}_{e_\vartheta}e_\varphi\) is the projection of \(e_\varphi\) onto \(e_\vartheta\), and the double bars denote magnitude. The polarizer can be visualized as a “magnitude window” for the light vector, and intensity (or ability to perform work) equals the square of magnitude. For a lossless beam-splitting polarizer, the regular channel follows that rule and the complementary channel the complementary rule

\begin{equation} \sin^2(\vartheta - \varphi) = 1 - (e_\vartheta\cdot e_\varphi)^2 = 1 - {\lVert \text{proj}_{e_\vartheta}e_\varphi\rVert}^2 \end{equation}

The impinging photon is polarized either \$e_1\$ or \$e_2\$. Therefore the intensity loss is in the regular channel of a beam splitter is one of

\begin{align*} (e_\vartheta\cdot e_1)^2 &= {\lVert\text{proj}_{e_\vartheta}e_1\rVert}^2 = \cos^2 \vartheta \\ (e_\vartheta\cdot e_2)^2 &= {\lVert\text{proj}_{e_\vartheta}e_2\rVert}^2 = \sin^2 \vartheta \\ \end{align*}

and for the respective complementary channel is

\begin{align*} 1-\cos^2\vartheta &= \sin^2\vartheta \\ 1-\sin^2\vartheta &= \cos^2\vartheta \end{align*}

and so the rule for a complementary channel is simply the rule for the regular channel, but with the roles of \$e_1\$ and \$e_2\$ reversed.
